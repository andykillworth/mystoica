Symbolic Notes
A — The Threshold Value
Gross's charge sits precisely on the numeral fence between petty and felony theft, a dollar figure engineered by statute to be a no-man's-land. He did not steal a small thing or a large one; he stepped into the bracket itself.
B — The Second Skin
A sweater is a promise of containment, warmth held against the body. When the promise fails at the level of fabric, the garment becomes the very hazard it was sewn to prevent — protection reversing its polarity.
C — The Broken Fence-Line
A cow on a highway is a boundary already dissolved before the vehicle ever arrives — enclosure failing first, collision only completing what the fence had already confessed.

Value, warmth, and enclosure are three different containers, and on this dispatch day all three ruptured at once — proof that thresholds do not fail in isolation, they fail in sympathy.

Threeness of the exhibits collapses into a repeated twoness underneath — the pattern doubling back on itself, as if every threshold that breaks does so in pairs, one visible and one hidden in the paperwork.

Working Theory — The Adjacency Clause

The Adjacency Clause holds that no dispatch is ever filed alone. Every report entered into a public record on a given day is quietly bonded to whatever else crossed a desk that same week — not by cause, but by custody. The three filings gathered here were never meant to touch: a shoplifting charge in Daviess County, a flammability recall out of a floor in Lower Manhattan, a cow struck on a two-lane highway in the Willamette foothills. But custody does not require intention. It requires only that a threshold be crossed, logged, and time-stamped near enough to another threshold crossing that the two begin, in the record if nowhere else, to answer to each other.

The Cascade

Trace the custody chain forward and the three ruptures resolve into a single sequence of failed containment.

1

Owensboro, Kentucky — Saturday: Jeffery Gross crosses the statutory threshold at the 1100 block of Pennbrooke Avenue, and the theft is logged inside the bracket the law built for exactly this ambiguity.

2

Benton County, Oregon — Eighteen days earlier, on July 2, a fence-line already failing lets a cow onto Kings Valley Highway — the first and quietest of the three enclosures to give way, filed and nearly forgotten by the time Gross is booked.

3

New York, New York — On July 16, in a compliance office above Lower Manhattan, Madewell's own paperwork admits what the fabric already knew — two sweater lines fail the standard meant to keep warmth from becoming fire.

4

By July 20 the three filings sit in the same eighteen-day custody window, never cross-referenced, never meant to be read together, and yet each one a record of a boundary that was supposed to hold and did not.

5

The Adjacency Clause does not explain why; it only notes that thresholds, once they start failing nearby in time, rarely fail alone.

The record does not ask what connects a shoplifter, a sweater, and a cow. It only asks what crossed the line, and when — and today, three things did.
