Symbolic Notes
A — The Voucher
A meal ticket is a promise of sustenance made portable, made paper. When it disappears from an office built for leisure, it is not food that is stolen but the guarantee of food — a future meal converted into an absence.
B — The Dresser
A drawer is a small architecture of trust: it holds clothing because we assume it will not fall. Ten thousand and forty units, unanchored, become ten thousand and forty unkept promises of containment.
C — The Jerky
Preserved meat is meant to be a stable, legible food. When wheat hides silently inside teriyaki flavoring, the label itself becomes an act of concealment — a container lying about what it contains.

Each exhibit is a vessel — a voucher, a drawer, a wrapper — that was trusted to hold something reliably, and each one, on the same news day, is revealed to have failed at exactly that task.

Working Theory — The Adjacency Clause

Under the Adjacency Clause, any three filings that surface within the same rolling cycle are presumed to share a hidden custody chain, regardless of geography or agency of origin — proximity in the record is itself a form of evidence. Today's chain moves from a stolen credit for food, to a container built to hold what is bought with such credit, to a wrapper that lied about what food it truly held. The Clause does not ask us to prove causation. It asks only that we notice when three separate systems of trust fail on the same calendar day, and to read that as a single fracture wearing three names.

The Cascade

Traced in custodial order, the day's failures move from the missing token, to the false wrapper, to the collapsing container meant to hold what both should have secured.
