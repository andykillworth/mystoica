Symbolic Notes
A — The Overturned Can
The bear does not break the law of the town; it obeys the older law of hunger. The trash can is a boundary marker, and its collapse is the record of appetite recognizing no fence.
B — The Fall Possibly Dangerous
A body meets a threshold it did not choose — a step, a floor, a circle named for landing — and the dispatch code freezes the instant between upright and undone.
C — The Swallowed Light
A toy built to project light outward is recalled because its true light — three button cells — can travel the wrong direction, inward, past the mouth, into the dark it was meant to illuminate.

Each exhibit is a crossing: an animal into a bin, a body into a fall, a light into a throat — three proofs that boundaries exist only until something wants what is on the other side badly enough.

Working Theory — The Adjacency Clause

The Adjacency Clause holds that no record exists in true isolation — that any two filings logged within the same rolling news cycle share a hidden custody, passed hand to hand like evidence nobody signed for. Here the clause tightens: a boundary breached by appetite in Alaska, a boundary breached by gravity in Missouri, a boundary breached by battery in a nameless Amazon warehouse. The Clause does not claim causation. It claims custody — that once three crossings are logged within hours of one another, they become bound by more than coincidence, they become a single case file scattered across three jurisdictions that never compared notes.

What was contained did not stay contained; that is the only verdict the record permits.
