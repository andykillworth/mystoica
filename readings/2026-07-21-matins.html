Working Theory — The Adjacency Clause

Under the Adjacency Clause, no incident is filed in true isolation — each blotter entry sits within a fixed radius of some other unremarked failure occurring in the same rolling week, and the address numerals are read as coordinates rather than coincidence. Here, the '400' recurs across two unconnected municipalities as though the same block-number were being tested twice for its capacity to hold something in. The clause holds that once a numeral repeats across independently filed public records, the records must be read as custodial handoffs of a single unresolved condition, passed from jurisdiction to jurisdiction until someone finally logs the loss correctly.

The Cascade

Traced forward, the custody of the unheld thing moves in five steps:

1

Salisbury, North Carolina — Something stops between 9:30 p.m. and 5:50 a.m., and no one is present to log the exact moment it happens — only the block number, 400, survives as a marker of where.

2

Greencastle, Indiana — Two days later, at the same block number, a leak is discovered only in its aftermath — the fire department arrives to find not a hazard but its residue, already spread across the road.

3

The pattern moves from address to inventory: 211,000 sandals, already distributed and worn since January, are found retroactively to have been failing the entire time they were on children's feet.

4

Twenty-three reports of the failure surface before the recall — the first small, countable acknowledgments that something ornamental was never structurally sound.

5

The custody chain closes on the numeral itself: 400 becomes 4, 211,000 becomes 4, and the record insists that whatever moved between Salisbury and Greencastle has now been formally re-filed as a consumer hazard, its true location no longer an address but a digit.

What was once a silence at 400 Prescot is now, four days later, a warning label — the loss has simply changed its jurisdiction.
