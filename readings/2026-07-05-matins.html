*Three unrelated records surfaced within seventy-two hours of one another, each describing an object suspended between what it was and what the state was willing to call it.*

Exhibit A — Filed
Waukesha, Wisconsin

A man visited the police department lobby to discuss applying for a permit to have his pet skunk's scent glands surgically removed, citing paperwork from the Department of Natural Resources; an officer noted that under Municipal Ordinance 33.01 sub 7, skunks are not listed among prohibited animals, and advised him to carry his permits on walks.

Established No. 21827 (Oklahoma distribution)

FSIS issued a public health alert for STREET'S BEEF Jerky Teriyaki Flavor, produced across eleven lot codes between August 7, 2025 and July 1, 2026, containing undeclared wheat; the product was shipped to three small Oklahoma retailers — Bordwine Hay, Feed & Hardware, Grassroots Farm Store, and Bromide Trading Co.

Exhibit C — Held
Boulder City, Nevada

A single-vehicle crash on Veterans Memorial Drive scattered roughly 200 feet of glass across both northbound lanes; the 65-year-old driver was found bleeding behind the wheel, armed, and her gun was taken into custody for 'safekeeping' — the call to Public Works came in at 6:42 p.m., the street sweeper did not arrive until 7:27 p.m.

Symbolic Notes
A — The Unlisted Animal
The skunk is not outlawed, only unmentioned — its danger neutralized not by statute but by the absence of a category. It walks the town wearing its permit like a leash made of paper.
B — The Undeclared Grain
Wheat hidden inside dried meat is a smaller thing hiding inside a larger, more durable one — a secret built to outlast its container, discovered only by a label review eleven months after the first lot left the smokehouse.
C — The Held Weapon
A firearm taken not as evidence but 'for safekeeping' belongs to no case file — it exists in a custody with no verdict attached, the way glass exists on a road for forty-five minutes before anyone is dispatched to sweep it.

Each exhibit is a thing the record could not finish processing: a permit advised but not granted, a recall issued for a product 'no longer available,' a weapon held but never charged. The state's hand closes around each object without ever making a final fist.

The digit 9 surfaces twice — once from the jerky's opening lot code, once from the forty-five idle minutes on the Nevada roadway.

Nine is the number of a cycle awaiting closure, not completion — fitting, since both nines mark intervals where something contaminated or dangerous sat in the open, unresolved, waiting for someone official to arrive.

Working Theory — The Adjacency Clause

The Adjacency Clause holds that no record is ever truly isolated: any two entries filed within the same rolling week begin, by the simple fact of proximity, to exchange properties across the gap between them. The skunk's suppressed scent, the jerky's suppressed grain, and the gun's suspended custody are not three separate acts of concealment — they are one continuous motion of the state's hand, closing over different materials in the same reporting cycle, each time calling the closure something other than what it is.

The Cascade

Trace the custody chain forward and the three exhibits resolve into a single gesture repeated in three jurisdictions.

1

Waukesha, Wisconsin — A permit is requested for an animal whose danger is chemical, not legal — the ordinance is silent, so the silence itself becomes the permit.

2

Est. No. 21827, Oklahoma distribution — A grain hides inside a preserved meat for eleven months before a routine label review finds it — the concealment outlives its own manufacture.

3

Boulder City, Nevada — A crash deposits 200 feet of glass on a public road; the driver's weapon is lifted from the scene and held, not booked — evidence that refuses to become a case.

4

Across all three records, forty-five minutes pass before anyone with authority arrives to resolve what has already happened — the skunk's walk, the jerky's shelf life, the glass on the asphalt all sit in that same unattended interval.

5

The interval closes not with a verdict but with a euphemism — 'advised,' 'no longer available for purchase,' 'safekeeping' — three words performing the same function: they end the paperwork without ending the fact.

*Nothing here was ever charged, recalled in full, or descented — only filed, which the record insists is the same as resolved.*
