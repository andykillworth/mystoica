Symbolic Notes
A — The Skunk
A scent kept for defense, legally stripped so it may walk beside its owner without warning anyone. It is the animal made harmless by permit — the threat de-fanged but still permitted to exist.
B — The Glass
Two hundred feet of a windshield scattered into the road becomes a field no one can walk without bleeding. The weapon in the driver's hand is confiscated not as evidence of guilt but as an act of custody — held, not condemned.
C — The Wheat
An allergen sealed inside meat, undeclared, waiting in eleven lot codes for a body that cannot tell the difference until it is too late. The hidden ingredient always travels under the name of something else.

Each exhibit is a record of something dangerous rendered invisible — descented, swept, or mislabeled — and each required an authority to intervene before the hidden thing could complete its arc.

The digit 7 surfaces twice — once in the sweeper's arrival, once in the ordinance subsection that frees the skunk from prohibition.

Seven is the number of the interval, the gap between the call and the correction; it names the lag every hidden thing is given before someone comes to sweep, declare, or confiscate it.

Working Theory — The Adjacency Clause

The Adjacency Clause holds that records filed within the same seventy-two-hour window, regardless of subject or jurisdiction, are bound by a shared undercurrent — as if the archive itself exhales once a day and everything caught in that breath inherits a common pulse. Here the pulse is concealment: a gland removed, a windshield turned to gravel, an allergen sealed in teriyaki. Each is a small, local failure of disclosure, corrected only after the fact by a permit, a confiscation, or a public alert. The Clause does not claim causation. It claims that concealment, once filed in triplicate across three unrelated counties, becomes its own weather system.

The Cascade

Trace the custody chain and the sequence declares itself without needing help.

All three records converge on the same unspoken clause: what is dangerous is not destroyed, only relabeled, permitted, or swept — and the sweeping always arrives late.
